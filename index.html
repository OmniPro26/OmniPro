Abstract

Omni-proactive streaming video understanding requires a model to autonomously decide when to speak and actively produce responses based on visual or auditory signals while processing a continuous video stream, rather than relying solely on visual triggers. Despite the rapid proliferation of streaming models targeting this capability, how to systematically evaluate them remains an open question: existing proactive benchmarks depend almost exclusively on visual triggers, failing to distinguish omni-modal models from vision-only counterparts; they adopt polling or fixed-timestamp paradigms that leave genuine autonomous timing untested; and their task diversity is narrow, mostly limited to alerting.

To jointly address these limitations, we present OmniProact-Bench, the first comprehensive benchmark for omni-proactive streaming video understanding. OmniProact-Bench comprises 2,700 human-reviewed samples spanning 9 sub-tasks and 3 cognitive levels that collectively map onto 6 fundamental video understanding capabilities, with 84% of samples depending on audio information. We further propose a dual-mode evaluation protocol: a GT Probe mode that tests temporal boundary sensitivity via pre/post paired probing, and an Online mode requiring frame-by-frame autonomous decision-making with multi-trigger support and over-trigger penalties.

Evaluation of 11 mainstream models reveals that current omni models benefit from audio yet differ markedly in their utilization ability; performance degrades substantially as the video stream progresses, exposing a fundamental deficiency in sustained attention; and non-speech sound perception remains a shared bottleneck across all architectures.

Representative samples from each sub-task showing the user instruction (Q) and expected proactive responses (A) with trigger times.

IEA – Instant Event Alert 🎧 Audio Required

Q: Let me know if the owner asks the dog if he's talking back.

A [00:08]: "The owner just asked Devin, 'Are you talking back to me?'" speech

RSM – Realtime State Monitor 🎧 Audio Required

Q: Monitor the background music and let me know whenever it starts or stops.

A [00:14]: "The music stopped — it went from playing to silence." sound

A [02:11]: "Music started — it went from silence to playing." sound

CC – Cumulative Counting 🎧 Audio Required

Q: Count how many times the narrator says the word 'game'.

A [00:10]: "The narrator just mentioned a 'four-quarter game'." speech

A [00:22]: "He says this game is important for getting the team back on track." speech

ETG – Explicit Target Grounding 🎧 Audio Required

Q: When the host says 'always' during his sign-off, tell me where his raised index finger is.

A [04:13]: "The host just said 'always' — his raised index finger is in the top-left." visual+speech

SCA – Semantic Condition Alert 🎧 Audio Required

Q: Alert me whenever the speaker mentions a specific number or statistic.

A [00:23]: "Kevin mentions over a thousand friends attended the bash." speech

A [00:26]: "He notes that over 60 students made a spiritual commitment." speech

SSI – Sequential Step Instruction 🎧 Audio Required

Q: I'm following along with this tutorial to change my road tire. Guide me through each step.

A [00:18]: "Open your brake calipers, then untighten the quick-release lever." visual+speech

A [00:29]: "Deflate the tube by pressing the valve. Use tire levers to pry the bead." visual+speech

Key Findings

  • Audio utilization varies widely: A+V gains range from +2.4 to +11.1 across omni models, revealing divergent audio encoding capabilities.
  • Temporal degradation: Models retain only 37% of early-segment performance on late triggers (180s+); online streaming models nearly fail entirely.
  • Non-speech sound is the weakest link: All models perform worst on visual+sound triggers (15.3–22.3%), highlighting a shared bottleneck.
  • Online mode adds fundamental difficulty: The coupled challenge of when-to-speak and what-to-say causes severe degradation on generation tasks.
