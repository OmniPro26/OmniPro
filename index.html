Abstract

Omni-proactive streaming video understanding requires a model to autonomously decide when to speak and actively produce responses based on visual or auditory signals while processing a continuous video stream, rather than relying solely on visual triggers. Despite the rapid proliferation of streaming models, how to systematically evaluate them remains an open question: existing proactive benchmarks depend almost exclusively on visual triggers; they adopt polling or fixed-timestamp paradigms that leave genuine autonomous timing untested; and their task diversity is narrow.

We present OmniProact-Bench, the first comprehensive benchmark for this task. It comprises 2,700 human-reviewed samples spanning 9 sub-tasks and 3 cognitive levels (6 capabilities), with 84% audio dependency. We propose a dual-mode protocol: GT Probe (pre/post paired probing) and Online (frame-by-frame autonomous decision-making with multi-trigger support and over-trigger penalties).

Evaluation of 11 mainstream models reveals that omni models differ markedly in audio utilization; performance degrades substantially over time; and non-speech sound perception remains a shared bottleneck.

Key Findings

  • Audio utilization varies widely: A+V gains range from +2.4 to +11.1 across omni models.
  • Temporal degradation: Models retain only 37% of early-segment performance on late triggers (180s+).
  • Non-speech sound is the weakest link: All models perform worst on visual+sound triggers (15.3–22.3%).
  • Online mode adds fundamental difficulty: The coupled challenge of when-to-speak and what-to-say severely degrades generation tasks.
